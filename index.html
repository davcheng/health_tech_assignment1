Tools and Approach

Data was analyzed using excel to calculate averages and identify correlations (via the data analysis toolpack). Once trends were explored and identified, the data was plotted using D3 and C3, a reusable javascript wrapper for D3. This website styling was built using foundation for styling.

1. Go / No Go

Mean response time (Correct) vs. Mean Accuracy Rate

Based on the collected data, the first graph for Go/No Go delineates the tradeoff between response accuracy and response speed. As depicted in the graph, the test results indicate that a slower response time leads to an improvement in response accuracy. However, it seems that after 300 ms, the strength of correlation diminishes. This may be a result of the smaller sample size or hidden variables not accounted for in the test.

Mean response time (Incorrect) vs. Mean Accuracy Rate

The second go/no-go chart depicts a strong trend between the mean response time of incorrect responses and the mean accuracy rate. This chart illustrates another way to analyze response accuracy by analyzing the speed of incorrect responses. As illustrated, the slower the mean response time, the better the accuracy rate. This chart portrays a correlation between "trigger fingers" and incorrect responses.

Maximum response time vs. Mean Accuracy Rate

The final go/no-go chart compares the maximum response time versus the mean accuracy rate. As shown, the lowest maximum response time yields the worst accuracy rate (52%). Interestingly enough, although the response accuracy does improve as response times increase, there is an eventual tapering off after 563 ms, suggesting that after a certain threshold, the accuracy may not be influenced by response time (since human reaction times are estimated to be around 300 milliseconds). After a certain extent, the correlation breaks and other factors may be greater predictors of accuracy rate.

2. Balloon betting Analogue Risk Test (BART)

Mean Pumps Comparison (after explosion/after no explosion)

In the Balloon betting analogue risk test (BART), the first graph visualized depicts the comparison between number of pumps following an explosion versus the number following no explosion (collection). The results show that in almost all circumstances, the subject was more risk adverse following an explosion, with a resulting pump average of 2.87 post-explosion versus 3.77 post-no-explosion.

Number of Explosions vs. Range of Pumps

The above graph illustrates a nuanced example of user calibration. In the beginning, the user is unsure of how many explosions is considered "good" or "bad". After 7 explosions on the first attempt, the user lowers his risk-profile (highlighted by the low number of pumps), and after seeing a decreased number of explosions, increases his risk tolerance. Eventually, the user is able to calibrate to the game and increase the range of pumps larger than the number of explosions.

Standard Deviation vs. Mean Pumps

The graph of standard deviation and mean pumps illustrates the user's playing style. The first trial ran had very little standard deviation, due to initial apprehension caused by unfamiliarity with the game. The clustering of 6 trials around 3.3 pumps illustrates that, regardless of standard deviation, the user may tend to stick to a "strategy" (and balance out previous acts of "risk"), regardless of intentionality.

3. Delayed Discounting

Average Response (Decision) Time over Responses

The average response time per decision was charted for each decision type. While it is difficult to draw any direct correlations, it is interesting to note that each type took the longest for at least one of the response sections. This lack of a pattern may hint that there may be many hidden variables which impact decision time. Further trends are explored in the next two visualizations.

Average Response (Decision) Time per Decision Type

The chart above delineates the average response time for each type of decision. As illustrated, decisions with a 1 month duration took longer decide than those related to the 6 month timeframe. Additionally, decisions relating to money took longer to make than those relating to time off. This may hint at the notion that direct decisions are easier to make than indirect decisions (money, something easily quantifiable, versus time off, which is far more complex to quantify).

Average Response (Decision) Time over Responses

The above chart illustrates that the response time for making a decision gradually decreases over each decision. This may be caused by the fact that the user is gradually pushed to identify a "breaking point" that enables future decisions to be progressively easier.
